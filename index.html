⚛️ Chapter 1: Chemical Foundations

Chemistry is the study of matter, its properties, the changes it undergoes, and the energy associated with those changes.

Scientific Method: Observation → Hypothesis → Experiment → Theory (Model) → Natural Law

  • Law — summarizes WHAT happens (e.g., Law of Mass Conservation)
  • Theory — explains WHY it happens

SI Units: Mass = kg, Length = m, Time = s, Temperature = K, Amount = mol

Temperature Conversions:

K = °C + 273.15  |  °F = (9/5)°C + 32  |  °C = (°F − 32) × 5/9

Significant Figures Rules:

  • Non-zero integers always count
  • Leading zeros do NOT count
  • Captive zeros always count
  • Trailing zeros count only with decimal point
  • Exact numbers have infinite sig figs

Classification of Matter:

  • Pure Substance → Elements / Compounds
  • Mixtures → Homogeneous (uniform) / Heterogeneous (non-uniform)

Density: Density = Mass / Volume (solids: g/cm³, liquids: g/mL, gases: g/L)

⚛️ Chapter 2: Atoms, Molecules and Ions

Three Fundamental Laws:

  • Law of Mass Conservation (Lavoisier) — mass is neither created nor destroyed
  • Law of Definite Composition (Proust) — a compound always has the same mass ratio of elements
  • Law of Multiple Proportions (Dalton) — masses of one element combining with fixed mass of another are in small whole number ratios

Dalton's Atomic Theory: Matter is made of indivisible atoms; atoms of same element are identical; compounds form from atoms of different elements in small whole-number ratios.

Subatomic Particles:

  • Proton: mass ≈ 1 amu, charge = +1, in nucleus
  • Neutron: mass ≈ 1 amu, charge = 0, in nucleus
  • Electron: mass ≈ 0.00055 amu, charge = −1, outside nucleus

Electron discovered by J.J. Thomson (cathode ray tube). Charge measured by Millikan (e = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C). Neutron discovered by Chadwick.

Rutherford's Nuclear Model: Most of atom is empty space; tiny dense positive nucleus surrounded by electrons.

Atomic Notation: ᴬ_Z X where A = mass number (p + n), Z = atomic number (p)

Isotopes: Same element, same protons, different neutrons. Chemically identical but different masses.

Proton is 1840 times heavier than electron — a very common exam question!

Atomic mass = weighted average of naturally occurring isotopes.

Ions: Cations (+) lose electrons; Anions (−) gain electrons. K, L, M shells hold 2, 8, 8 electrons respectively → atomic number 18 (Argon) when all full.

🔢 Quantum Numbers & Electronic Structure

Four Quantum Numbers describe each electron:

  • n (Principal): n = 1, 2, 3… → energy level / shell
  • l (Azimuthal): l = 0 to (n−1) → subshell (s=0, p=1, d=2, f=3)
  • mₗ (Magnetic): mₗ = −l to +l → orbital orientation
  • mₛ (Spin): +½ or −½ → electron spin

Key rule: l ranges from 0 to (n−1), NOT to (n+1). The statement l = 1,2,3,(n+1) is INCORRECT — common exam trap!

Orbital Shapes: s = spherical; p = dumbbell (3 orientations); d = 5 orientations; f = 7 orientations

Max electrons per sublevel: s=2, p=6, d=10, f=14

Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle: Cannot simultaneously know exact position AND momentum of an electron.

Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons can have the same 4 quantum numbers.

Hund's Rule: Electrons fill degenerate orbitals singly before pairing.

Electron config — Arsenic (As, Z=33): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d¹⁰ 4p³

Nickel (Ni, Z=28): [Ar] 4s² 3d⁸

ns²np⁴ configuration = sulfur (S, Group 6A)

Cobalt (Co, Z=27): [Ar] 4s² 3d⁷ → 3 unpaired electrons → paramagnetic

Rubidium (Rb, Z=37): Has 18 p-electrons (2p⁶ + 3p⁶ + 4p⁶)

Letter 's' in azimuthal quantum number stands for sharp. The f-subshell has 7 degenerate orbitals.

⚖️ Chemical Equilibrium

Reversible reactions — reactants form products AND products reverse to reactants simultaneously.

At equilibrium: forward rate = reverse rate. Neither reaction stops — it's a dynamic equilibrium.

Equilibrium Constant Kc: For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ

  • Pure solids and liquids are NOT included in Kc expression
  • K only changes with temperature
  • K is NOT affected by concentration, pressure, or catalyst changes

Reaction Quotient Q: Same formula as K but uses initial/current concentrations (not equilibrium). If Q < K, reaction goes forward. If Q > K, reaction goes reverse.

Le Chatelier's Principle: System shifts to counteract stress.

  • Add reactant → shift right (more products)
  • Remove product → shift right
  • Increase pressure → shift toward fewer moles of gas
  • Increase temperature → shift toward endothermic direction
  • Catalyst → speeds up both directions equally, no shift in equilibrium position

Kp vs Kc: Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn where Δn = moles of gaseous products − moles of gaseous reactants

Relationship to Gibbs Energy: ΔG° = −RT ln K

For 2ICl(s) ⇌ I₂(s) + Cl₂(g): Since ICl and I₂ are solids, Kc = [Cl₂] only.

⚡ Reaction Kinetics

Rate of reaction — how fast reactants are consumed or products formed.

Rate Law: Rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ (m, n determined experimentally, NOT from stoichiometry)

Units of k: Depend on overall order. For Rate = k[A][B]² (3rd order) → units = L²·mol⁻²·s⁻¹

Half-life (first order): t₁/₂ = 0.693/k (constant, independent of initial concentration)

First-order integrated rate law: ln[A]t = ln[A]₀ − kt

Activation Energy (Ea): Minimum energy needed for reaction. Lower Ea = faster reaction.

Arrhenius Equation: k = A·e^(−Ea/RT)

Catalysts: Lower Ea; provide new pathway; do NOT change ΔG, ΔH, or Kc; actually participate in reaction.

For endothermic reaction with Ea = 50 kJ/mol, the reverse reaction Ea is greater than 50 kJ, and ΔE > 0 (energy absorbed).

Collision Theory: Reaction rate increases with concentration because collision frequency increases. Higher temp = more high-energy collisions.

Reaction Mechanisms: The slow step (rate-determining step) controls the overall rate law. A correct mechanism is often difficult to prove definitively.

For the reaction 2H₂S(g) + O₂(g) → 2S(s) + 2H₂O(l): Rate law CANNOT be determined from the balanced equation alone — must be determined experimentally.

🧪 Stoichiometry & Solutions

Molar mass — sum of atomic masses (in g/mol)

Moles: n = mass / molar mass

Molarity (M): M = moles solute / liters solution

Dilution: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂

Limiting reagent: Reactant that runs out first, determines how much product forms.

Percent composition: % element = (mass of element / molar mass of compound) × 100

Key formula weight calculations:
Al₂(SO₄)₃·18H₂O = 666.4 g/mol
Alanine (C₃H₇NO₂) = 89 g/mol → 159g = 1.78 mol

Isotope abundance calculation: Weighted average: Σ(fraction × mass)

Light frequency: c = λν where c = 3×10⁸ m/s

Energy of photon: E = hν = hc/λ where h = 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s

Bohr Model: Electrons in fixed circular orbits. Lowest energy = closest to nucleus. Electrons ABSORB energy to jump up, EMIT energy to fall down.
